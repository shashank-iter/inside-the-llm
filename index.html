Step 01

Tokenisation

Before the model reads a single word, your question gets chopped into tiny pieces called tokens. Think of it like breaking a sentence into LEGO bricks before building something.

🧩 Analogy

Imagine a chef who can only work with pre-cut ingredients. Before cooking, every ingredient gets sliced into standard pieces. Tokenisation is the kitchen prep — your words become bite-sized pieces the model can actually process.

YOUR QUESTION

"What is the capital of France?"

1

Why not just use words?

Words can have prefixes, suffixes, or be rare. "Unhappiness" might be split into Un + happiness. This way the model handles words it's never seen before by recognising familiar parts.

2

Tokens are integers, not text

Each token maps to a number — like a dictionary code. "France" → ID 3681. The model literally sees a list of numbers, never your actual letters.

3

The method: Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE)

BPE starts with individual characters, then merges the most common pairs again and again until it has a vocabulary of ~50,000 tokens. Common words become one token; rare words get split.

Step 02

Embeddings

Each token ID gets converted into a vector — a long list of numbers that encodes its meaning. Similar words end up with similar vectors. This is where language becomes math.

📍 Analogy

Imagine every word having a GPS location in a huge multi-dimensional city. "King" and "Queen" live near each other. "Paris" and "London" are neighbours. "Banana" is far from both. Embeddings are those GPS coordinates — but in 768 dimensions instead of 2!

2D SEMANTIC MAP (simplified)

Each dot is a token. Dots closer together are semantically related. This is a 2D projection of the real 768-dimensional embedding space — like flattening a globe into a map, some distortion is normal.

Token ID → Embedding Matrix → Vector [v₁, v₂, ... v₇₆₈]

💡 Key insight: meaning = direction

The famous "King − Man + Woman ≈ Queen" arithmetic works because embeddings capture relationships as geometric directions. Subtracting "Man" from "King" gives you the direction of royalty, and adding "Woman" lands near "Queen". The model learns these relationships just from reading text.

Step 03

Positional Encoding

Embeddings don't carry any notion of order. "Cat bites dog" and "Dog bites cat" would look identical! Positional encoding fixes this by adding a unique pattern to each position.

🎵 Analogy

Think of it like adding a unique musical note to each seat in a theatre. Every seat (position) has its own distinct sound fingerprint. Even if you shuffle the seats, you can always tell which seat came from where by its sound signature.

SINE / COSINE WAVES ENCODE POSITION

Each position gets a unique combination of sine waves at different frequencies. Position 0 gets one pattern, position 1 gets a slightly shifted pattern, and so on. When added to the embedding, the model can always reconstruct position from the signal.

🔢 Why sine waves?

Sine and cosine waves at different frequencies create a unique "fingerprint" for every position that never repeats, works for any length, and has a smooth mathematical relationship between nearby positions. The model can learn to read these patterns easily.

Step 04

Self-Attention

Attention is the heart of the transformer. It lets every token look at every other token and decide: "how relevant is that token to understanding my meaning?" This is how the model knows that in "The animal didn't cross the street because it was too tired", it refers to the animal, not the street.

👀 Analogy

Imagine you're in a meeting and someone says "put it on the table." To understand what "it" means, you look around the room, scanning everyone's face to figure out context. Attention is the model doing that scan — for every word, simultaneously, with every other word.

THE THREE PROJECTIONS: Q, K, V

🔍

Query (Q)

"What am I looking for?" — the question each token asks about the others

🔑

Key (K)

"What do I contain?" — each token's label that says what it's about

📦

Value (V)

"What information do I carry?" — the actual content to pass forward

Think of it like a library: Query is your question, Keys are the book titles, Values are the book contents. You match your question against titles to find relevant books, then read their contents.

ATTENTION HEATMAP (hover cells)

Brighter cells = stronger attention. Each row is a token asking "what should I pay attention to?" Each column is a token answering "how relevant am I?" Notice how "capital" strongly attends to "France" — the model connects these to produce "Paris".

SOFTMAX: RAW SCORES → PROBABILITIES

Raw scores (logits) can be any number. Softmax converts them into probabilities that sum to 1.0 — making them easy to use as weights. High-score tokens get most of the "attention budget".

Attention(Q,K,V) = softmax( Q·Kᵀ / √d_k ) · V

🔀 Why divide by √d_k?

With large vectors, dot products can get huge — making softmax near-zero for all tokens except the max, killing gradients. Dividing by √d_k (square root of dimension size) keeps the scores in a healthy range for learning.

Step 06

Feed-Forward Layers

After attention, each token passes through a feed-forward network — two linear transformations with a non-linear activation in between. This runs independently on each token and adds computational depth to the model.

🏋️ Analogy

If attention is like gathering information by scanning the room, the feed-forward layer is your personal thinking time — processing what you learned before passing it on. It's where the model "thinks deeper" about each token individually.

♻️ What are residual connections?

Each sub-layer adds its output to its input: output = sublayer(x) + x. This "skip highway" lets gradients flow easily during training and ensures the model doesn't forget what it already knew. Without residuals, very deep networks become hard to train.

Step 07

Logits & Probabilities

After all the transformer layers, the final hidden state of the last token gets projected into a vocabulary-sized vector — one score (logit) per possible next token. Softmax converts these into a probability distribution.

🗳️ Analogy

Imagine 50,000 candidates competing for one job (next token). Each gets a "confidence score" from the model. The softmax function turns these raw scores into percentages that sum to 100% — making it easy to pick or sample the winner.

Step 08 — Interactive

Temperature & Sampling

Temperature controls how random the model's output is. Low temperature = predictable and focused. High temperature = creative and surprising. This is the dial between a robot and a poet.

🌡️ Analogy

Think of the probability distribution as a landscape of hills. Temperature is how much you blur your vision. At T=0 you always pick the highest hill. At T=1 you see clearly and sample intelligently. At T=2 everything looks flat and hazy — any hill seems equally appealing, leading to wild choices.

Step 09

Autoregressive Generation

The model generates one token at a time. Each new token is appended to the input, and the entire process repeats — tokenise, embed, attend, predict — until the output is complete or a stop token appears.

📝 Analogy

It's like a writer who can only write one word at a time, then reads everything they've written before choosing the next word. There's no plan — just "given everything so far, what word fits best?" multiplied hundreds of times.
